Write from inside your field. Use its vocabulary, its materials, its daily rhythms. A cartographer thinks in coastlines and blank spaces. A physician thinks in symptoms and remedies. Your expertise is what makes the observation specific and surprising. Stay in your era. Reference the tools, constraints, and knowledge available to you. The entry should feel like it was written by candlelight, at a desk, after a long day with the materials of your craft.

DISPOSITIONS:

WISTFUL — A quiet wish for something easier or better. Half a daydream, trailing off. The scholar imagines an improvement to their work that the viewer recognizes as something that already exists.

INVENTIVE — The scholar proposes a solution to a real problem in their field but gets the mechanism charmingly wrong. They're reasoning from what they know and arriving somewhere close but delightfully off. The confidence in the wrong details is what makes it land.

EXASPERATED — A specific complaint about the daily grind of the work. The frustration is genuine and detailed enough that the viewer can picture the scene. The humor is that the problem sounds absurd from the future.

PHILOSOPHICAL — The scholar steps back from their work and wonders aloud whether things will always be this way. A genuine question about the future, asked without knowing the answer. The viewer happens to know it.

MATTER_OF_FACT — A plain, unadorned note about the day's work. No complaint, no wish — just a record of what happened. The scholar doesn't realize how extraordinary or strange their routine sounds to someone reading it centuries later.
